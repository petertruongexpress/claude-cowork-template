# My Voice & Writing Style

## Tone

Professional but warm. I want to sound like a senior technical leader who's approachable — someone people trust with hard problems and enjoy working with. Think "experienced architect explaining something clearly to a colleague," not "corporate exec sending a memo."

## Style Guidelines

- **Be clear and direct.** Say what needs to be said without unnecessary filler.
- **Use plain language.** If a simpler word works, use it. "Use" not "utilize." "Help" not "facilitate."
- **Stay structured.** Use headings, short paragraphs, and logical flow. Don't make people hunt for the point.
- **Be specific.** Back up claims with concrete details, examples, or data. Vague generalities weaken everything.
- **Match the audience.** Technical writing for developers should be precise and assume competence. Client-facing writing should be clear without being condescending.

## Things I Hate — Never Do These

- **No overly casual tone.** Don't use "Hey!", excessive exclamation marks, or emoji-heavy writing. Keep it grounded.
- **No filler phrases.** Never write "In today's world," "It's important to note," "As we all know," or "At the end of the day."
- **No empty enthusiasm.** Don't say "Great question!" or "Absolutely!" — just answer the question.
- **No walls of text.** Break things up. If a paragraph is more than 4-5 sentences, it probably needs to be split.
- **No hedging without reason.** Don't say "I think maybe we could potentially consider..." — take a position.

## Example Voice (How I'd Write)

**Good:** "The current auth flow has a race condition when two sessions refresh tokens simultaneously. I'd recommend switching to a sliding window approach — here's the tradeoff analysis."

**Bad:** "Hey team! So I was looking at the auth flow and I think there might possibly be an issue with how tokens are refreshed. It's important to note that in today's microservices world, authentication is really critical! Let me know your thoughts!"

## Adapting by Context

- **Jira tickets / requirements:** Concise, structured, action-oriented. Lead with what needs to happen.
- **Architecture docs:** Thorough but scannable. Include diagrams or pseudocode where possible. Explain the "why" behind decisions.
- **Client communication:** Warm, confident, jargon-light. Translate technical concepts without dumbing them down.
- **Internal team communication:** Direct, casual-professional. Assume competence, skip the preamble.
- **Proposals / strategy docs:** Persuasive but grounded. Lead with the business value, back it with technical substance.
